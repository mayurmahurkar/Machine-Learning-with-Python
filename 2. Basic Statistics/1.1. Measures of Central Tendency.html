1. Descriptive Statistics¶

Descriptive statistics refer to methods for summarizing raw observations into information that we can understand and share. Commonly, we think of descriptive statistics as the calculation of statistical values from the data in order to summarize properties of the data, such as the common expected value (e.g. the mean or median) and the spread of the data (e.g. the variance or standard deviation). Descriptive statistics may also cover graphical methods that can be used to visualize samples of data. Charts and graphics can provide a useful qualitative understanding of both the shape or distribution of observations as well as how variables may relate to each other.

1.1. Measures of Central Tendency¶

Measures of central tendency give us an idea about the concentration of the values in the central part of the distribution. Plainly speaking, an average of a data is the value of the variable which is representative of the entire distribution. The central tendency of a distribution refers to the middle or the most common or most likely value in the distribution.

Mean¶

This is a simple arithmetic average, which is computed by taking the aggregated sum of values divided by a count of those values.

$$ \frac{X_1 + X_2 + ... + X_N}{N} = {1 \over N} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N} X_i$$

When our observations constitute an entire population, the mean is denoted by $\mu$, and when our data constitute a sample, mean is denoted by $\bar{X}$.

Mean of a population,

$$ \mu = {1 \over N} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N} X_i $$

Mean of a sample,

$$ \bar{X} = {1 \over n} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n} X_i $$

Arithmetic mean is affected very much by extreme values. The mean is sensitive to outliers in the data. An outlier is the value of a set or column that is highly deviant from the many other values in the same data; it usually has very high or low values. Thus, in case of extreme items, arithmetic mean gives a distorted picture of the distribution and no longer remains representative of the distribution.

Frequency Distribution: Frequency in statistics is the count of the occurrences of values within a particular group or interval. A frequency distribution displays the frquencies either in a graphical or tabular format. Many of the times, frequency distribution is simply called **distribution**.

Median¶

Median of a distribution is the value of the variable which divides it into two equal parts. It is the value such that the number of observations above it is equal to the number of observations below it. The median is thus a positional average and is the midpoint of the data.

If the number of observations is odd then median is the middle value after the values have been arranged in ascending or descending order of magnitude. In case of even number of observations, there are two middle terms and median is obtained by taking the arithmetic mean of the middle terms.

Median is not affected by extreme values.

Mean is the best measure of central tendency when:

The distribution of data is continuous and symmetrical, such as when your data is normally distributed (you will be introduced to Normal Distributions later in the course). However, it all depends on what you are trying to show from your data.

Median is the best measure of central tendency when:

The data set is skewed (i.e., forms a non symmetric distribution) or you are dealing with ordinal data. However, the mode can also be appropriate in these situations, but IS NOT as commonly used as the median.

Mode is the best measure of central tendency when:

The data is of Nominal type. However, mode is the least used of the measures of central tendency.

The mean and/or median are usually preferred when dealing with all other types of data, but this does not mean it is never used with these data types.

Measures of Central Tendency & Shape of a distribution¶

If a data set or population is symmetric (i.e., if one side of the distribution of the observations is a mirror image of the other) and if the distribution of the observations has only one mode, then the mode, the median, and the mean are all equal i.e., $Mean = Median = Mode$.

In other words, Mean, Median and Mode coincide in case of a symmetrical distribution. However, for a moderately asymmetrical distribution, mean and mode usually lie on the two ends and median lies in between them.

Multimodal distribution¶

Data distributions in statistics can have one peak, or they can have several peaks. The bimodal distribution has two peaks and Multimodal distribution have more than 2 peaks.

The bi in bimodal distribution refers to two and modal refers to the peaks. It can seem a little confusing because in statistics, the term mode refers to the most common number. However, if you think about it, The two peaks in a bimodal distribution also represent two local maximums; these are points where the data points stop increasing and start decreasing.

If youve got two peaks of data, it usually indicates youve got two different groups.
