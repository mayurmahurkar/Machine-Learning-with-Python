1.2. Measures of Dipsersion (Spread)¶

Que: Consider the following two data sets:

set1 = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]

set2 = [4, 5, 5, 5, 6, 6, 6, 6, 7, 7, 7, 8]

Compute the mean, median, and mode of each of the two data sets.

from scipy import stats

set1 = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]
set2 = [4, 5, 5, 5, 6, 6, 6, 6, 7, 7, 7, 8]

mean1 = np.mean(set1)
mean2 = np.mean(set2)

median1 = np.median(set1)
median2 = np.median(set2)

Set 1 - Mean: 6.0 , Median: 6.0 , Mode : 6
Set 2 - Mean: 6.0 , Median: 6.0 , Mode : 6

As you see from your results, the two data sets have the same mean, the same median, and the same mode, all equal to 6. The two data sets also happen to have the same number of observations, $n = 12$. But the two data sets are different. What is the main difference between them?

The two data sets have the same central tendency (as measured by any of the three measures of centrality), but they have a different variability. In particular, we see that data set I have more variation than data set II. The values in set I are more spread out: they lie farther away from their mean than do those of set II.

Thus we see that the measures of central tendency are inadequate to give us a complete idea of the distribution. They must be supported and supplemented by some other measures. One such measure is Dispersion also called measure of variablility or simply spread of the data.

Measure of Dispersion (Spread): Literal meaning of dispersion is ‘scatteredness’. Dispersion is the variation in the data, and measures the inconsistencies in the value of variables in the data. Dispersion actually provides an idea about the spread rather than central values.

Range¶

The range is the difference between the maximum and minimum value of the data set.

The range of the observations in the above example is $Largest \, Number - Smallest \, Number$ i.e., $56 - 18 = 38$. The range of the data in set I is $11 - 1 = 10$, and the range of the data in set II is $8 - 4 = 4$. We see that, conforming with what we expect from looking at the two data sets, the range of set I is greater than the range of set II. Set I is more variable.

Interquartile Range¶

It is a measure of dispersion based on the upper quartile $Q_3$ and the lower quartile $Q_1$ i.e.,

$$ Interquartile \, Range = Q_3 - Q_1 $$

So what are these values $Q_3$ and $Q_1$ ? Lets see,

Quantiles:¶

These are simply identical fragments (subdivisions) of the data.

Quantiles covers:

  • quartiles
  • deciles
  • percentiles and so on.

These measures are calculated after arranging the data in ascending order.

Quartile:¶

The values which divide the given series of given data into four equal parts, when arranged in ascending or descending order are called quartiles. There are 3 such points denoted by $Q_1, Q_2, Q_3$ such that $Q_1 \le Q_2 \le Q_3$. Thus each quartile act as a marker of one-fourth of the data.

$Q_1$ known as the first quartile or lower quartile is the value which has 25% of the items of the distribution below it. $Q_2$, the second quartile has 50% of the observations below it and coincides with the median. $Q_3$, known as the third quartile or upper quartile has 75% of the observations below it.

Percentiles:¶

Percentiles are the values which divide the series into 100 equal parts, when arranged in ascending or descending order. There are 99 points denoted by $P_1,P_2,….,P_{99}$ such that $P_1,\le P_2 \le ….,\le P_{99}$. In the case of percentiles $P_{50}$ coincides with the median as the number of data points below the median is about 50 percent of the data.

Quartiles are often also expressed as percentiles.The first quartile is 25 percent of the data, the second quartile is 50 percent of the data, the third quartile is 75 percent of the data. The second quartile is also known as the median or 50th percentile or 5th decile.

Observe that for a data set:

  • $P_{ 25} = Q_1$
  • $P_{ 50} = Q_2$
  • $P_{ 75} = Q_3$

Mean Absolute Deviation¶

The Average Absolute Deviation, of a data set is the average of the absolute deviations from a central point. In the general form, the central point can be the mean, median, mode. The absolute values of the differences between the data points and their central tendency are totaled and divided by the number of data points.

$$ Avg. Absolute Deviation = {1 \over N} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N} |X_i - A| $$

Where $A$ is is usually mean, median or mode of the population.

If Mean, being the most used average is considered as the measure of central tendency then the above formula becomes

$$ MAD = {1 \over N} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N} |X_i - \bar X| $$

and is known as Mean Absolute Deviation

There are also two other, more commonly used measures of dispersion. These are the variance and the square root of the variance—the standard deviation.

Variance¶

The variance of a set of observations is the average squared deviation of the data points from their mean.

When our observations constitute an entire population, the variance is denoted by $\sigma^2$, and the averaging is done by dividing by $N$. (And $\sigma$ is the Greek letter sigma; we call the variance sigma squared.

$$\sigma^2 = {1 \over N} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N} (X_i - \mu)^2 $$

Where $\mu$ is the mean of the population.

Standard Deviation¶

The standard deviation of a set of observations is the (positive) square root of the variance of the set.

$$\sigma = \sqrt{{1 \over N} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N} (X_i - \mu)^2} $$

Why would we use the standard deviation when we already have its square, the variance?¶

The standard deviation is a more meaningful measure. The variance is the average squared deviation from the mean. It is squared because if we just compute the deviations from the mean and then averaged them, we get zero (prove this with any of the data sets). Therefore, when seeking a measure of the variation in a set of observations, we square the deviations from the mean; this removes the negative signs, and thus the measure is not equal to zero. The measure we obtain—the variance—is still a squared quantity; it is an average of squared numbers. By taking its square root, we “unsquare” the units and get a quantity denoted in the original units of the problem (e.g., dollars instead of dollars squared, which would have little meaning in most applications). The variance tends to be large because it is in squared units.

The variance of a distribution refers to how much on average that observations vary or differ from the mean value. It is useful to think of the variance as a measure of the spread of a distribution. A low variance will have values grouped around the mean (e.g. a narrow bell shape), whereas a high variance will have values spread out from the mean (e.g. a wide bell shape.)

When our observations constitute an entire population, the variance is denoted by $\sigma^2$, and the averaging is done by dividing by $N$.

Population Variance,

$$\sigma^2 = {1 \over N} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N} (X_i - \mu)^2 $$

Population Standard Deviation,

$$\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} = \sqrt{{1 \over N} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N} (X_i - \mu)^2} $$

When our data constitute a sample, the variance is denoted by $S^2$, and the averaging is done by dividing the sum of the squared deviations from the mean by $n - 1$.

Sample Variance,

$$ S^2 = {1 \over n-1} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n} (X_i - \bar X)^2 $$

Sample Standard Deviation,

$$ S = \sqrt{S^2} = \sqrt{{1 \over n-1} \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n} (X_i - \bar X)^2} $$

NOTE: Unless noted otherwise, we will assume that all our data sets are samples and do not constitute entire populations

Five-Number Summary¶

The Five-number summary, or 5-number summary for short can be used to describe the distribution of data samples for data with any distribution. The Five-number summary involves the calculation of 5 summary statistical quantities,namely:

  • Median i.e., the middle value in the sample, also called the 50th percentile or the 2nd quartile.
  • 1st Quartile i.e., The 25th percentile.
  • 3rd Quartile i.e., The 75th percentile.
  • Minimum: The smallest observation in the sample.
  • Maximum: The largest observation in the sample.

Boxplot¶

In descriptive statistics, a box plot or boxplot is a method for graphically depicting groups of numerical data through their quartiles. Box plots may also have lines extending from the boxes (whiskers) indicating variability outside the upper and lower quartiles, hence the terms box-and-whisker plot and box-and-whisker diagram.

A boxplot is a standardized way of displaying the distribution of data based on a five number summary. It can tell you about your outliers and what their values are. It can also tell you if your data is symmetrical, how tightly your data is grouped, and if and how your data is skewed.
